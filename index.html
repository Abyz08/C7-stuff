Exothermic Reactions
Exothermic reactions are those that release energy to the surroundings, typically in the form of heat. This release results in a temperature increase in the immediate environment. Common examples include combustion reactions, such as the burning of fuels, and neutralisation reactions between acids and bases.

Endothermic Reactions
In contrast, endothermic reactions absorb energy from the surroundings, leading to a temperature decrease in the environment. An example provided is the thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate to produce calcium oxide and carbon dioxide. These reactions are essential in various industrial processes and are explained with clear diagrams and explanations.

Energy Profiles
Energy profile diagrams visually represent the energy changes during chemical reactions. These diagrams show the energy levels of reactants and products, illustrating the activation energy required for a reaction to occur. The difference in energy between reactants and products determines whether a reaction is exothermic or endothermic.

Activation Energy
Activation energy is the minimum energy required to initiate a chemical reaction. Factors such as temperature, concentration, and surface area can influence the activation energy and, consequently, the rate of reaction. These factors are crucial in controlling and optimising chemical processes in various industries.

Applications and Importance
Understanding energy changes in chemical reactions is vital for numerous applications. For instance, exothermic reactions are harnessed in heat packs and combustion engines, while endothermic reactions are used in processes like photosynthesis and certain industrial applications.
